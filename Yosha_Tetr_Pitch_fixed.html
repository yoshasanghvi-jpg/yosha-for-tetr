01 · Before the ideas

Three things I noticed about Tetr

Before I wrote any of this, I read everything about Tetr. Three things stuck. Each one tells you what the job really takes, and they shaped everything below.

Noticed / 01

No campus means no defaults

A normal college gets its traditions for free, because the buildings never move. Tetr's campuses move every term. So anything you want students to hold onto has to fit in a carry-on.

→ rule: everything fits in hand luggage
Noticed / 02

Grading on revenue makes failure public

When you grade on revenue, a flop happens in front of everyone. The whole cohort sees the number. That's a bold way to run a school, and it means the help a student needs has to follow them, wherever they end up.

→ rule: care must cross borders with the cohort
Noticed / 03

Every cohort relearns the last one's lessons

Every cohort lands needing the same answers, which SIM to buy, which doctor to trust, which landlord to avoid. The early ones learned it all the hard way, and most of what they figured out is stuck in old group chats nobody new can get to.

→ rule: knowledge must outlive the batch that earned it

04 · From page to plan

The first ninety days

The ideas are the easy part. The hard part is the order you do them in. I'd listen before I build anything, do the cheapest wins first so people trust it, and make sure every experience keeps working after the cohort moves countries, even when I'm not around.

The Young Founders' LetterTETR · 2026
Edition I · The Founder

On building before you are ready

To the young founders reading this,

When I started TETR, the plan sounded reckless the moment I said it out loud. The idea was to take eighteen-year-olds, send them across the world, and ask them to build real companies before they had a degree in anything. Most people in education told me it wouldn't work, and about some of it, they were right.

What worked this year still surprises me. Students who had never sold anything closed real, paying customers in cities they'd landed in only days earlier. A few of them built things I'd have backed if they had walked into my office as strangers. That's the whole bet: you learn to build by building.

What didn't work, I'll be just as honest about. We moved too fast in a few places. We underestimated how heavy it is to start over in a new country every few months, and how much that takes out of a person even when the work is going well. We're still learning how to hold a group together while the ground keeps shifting under them, and I'd rather tell you that than pretend it's solved.

If you're deciding whether to start something young, here's my honest advice. You'll never feel ready. Ready is a story people tell themselves so they can keep waiting. The founders I back are the ones who start with whatever they have and fix it in public while everyone else is still getting set. Begin before you're ready, stay honest about what breaks, and keep going.

We're only two years in, and the first class graduates in 2028. I'll write to you every year until then, and after that too, with whatever we actually learn.

Pratham Mittal
Founder, TETR
Edition II · The Co-founder

What it takes to run a college that never stands still

To the young founders,

Pratham writes about the bet, so I'll write about the machine, because the machine is where most of the lessons actually live.

A normal college has one building and one calendar. We have a cohort that picks up its whole life every few months and lands in a new country with a new business to build. That reads well on a brochure. In practice it means visas, housing, partners, and a hundred small things all have to be ready in a city before the students arrive, every single time. When one of those breaks, students feel it on day one. We've had days like that, and we own them.

What we got right this year was the people we put around the students. The founders and operators who show up as mentors are the real curriculum, and a student learns more in one honest hour with someone who has built and lost than in a month of lectures. We leaned into that, and it worked.

Where we got it wrong was assuming the structure could be light. Young founders need freedom, but they also need a spine to push against. We added more of that this year, and the work got sharper for it.

If you're building anything, here's the unglamorous truth I'd pass on. The idea is maybe a tenth of it. The other nine tenths is doing ordinary things reliably, on time, when no one is watching. Founders who learn that early beat founders with better ideas who never learn it at all.

Tarun Gangwar
Co-founder, TETR
Edition III · The Student

From the inside, by a student

To whoever is reading this and deciding,

I'll tell you the parts the website can't.

The hardest part of all of this is the leaving. Every few months you build a life in a city, a routine, people you actually like, and then you pack it into a suitcase and start over somewhere you've never been. The first time it happens, it knocks you sideways. By the third, you've learned something about yourself that I don't think you can learn sitting still.

The best thing is that the work is real. I sold to actual customers in a country I'd landed in that week. I've failed in front of real people, which is worse and better than failing an exam, because you remember it and you fix it. I've sat across from founders I used to only read about and asked them the questions I actually had.

I'd only tell you to come if you genuinely want to be uncomfortable on purpose. If you want a safe four years, this isn't it. If you want to find out what you're capable of when nobody is holding your hand, I haven't found a faster way.

I'm not going to pretend every term was good, because some of them were genuinely lonely. But I'm a different person than the one who applied, and I'd do it again without thinking twice.

A student
